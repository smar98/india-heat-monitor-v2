§ 2TODAY’S RESULT · FORECAST

Today, the recommended morning and evening hours are forecast to cross the heat-stress limit in … of … cities.

What counts as an overlooked hour. All three at once:
Outside 11am–5pm: an hour the plans point workers to Sun up: daylight, real solar load Over the limit: forecast WBGT at or above the NIOSH work limit for the selected workload

WBGT (wet-bulb globe temperature) is the standard occupational heat-stress measure: one number folding together heat, humidity, sun and wind, estimated here from public forecast data. The NIOSH limit slides with how hard the work is; heavier work makes more body heat, so its limit is lower. Hot nights over the limit are counted separately, outside the headline.

Why WBGT rather than air temperature?

Air temperature is what IMD’s heat-wave declarations use, and it misses most of what heat does to a working body: humidity blocks sweat from evaporating, sun adds radiant load, wind removes some of it. WBGT folds all four into one number and is the international standard for occupational heat (ISO 7243), used by militaries, sports bodies and workplace regulators worldwide, and by Indian occupational-heat researchers. India’s official heat warnings don’t use it, which is part of the gap this page examines.

Why a US limit (NIOSH) on an Indian page?

Because there is no Indian equivalent to borrow: none of the plans audited here, and no Indian statute we could find, publishes a numerical heat-exposure limit for outdoor work. NIOSH’s recommended exposure limit is the most widely cited published occupational limit, its formula is public (the ACGIH and ISO threshold tables are copyrighted and paywalled), and Indian field studies have measured real heat strain in Indian workers at around these WBGT levels (Venugopal et al. 2016). This page uses the acclimatized-worker line, the more permissive one, which credits Indian outdoor workers’ heat adaptation rather than treating them as unaccustomed to it.

Why test 11am–5pm when the national line says 12–3?

11am–5pm is the union of every window in the audited plans (Odisha starts at 11am, Gujarat’s runs to 5pm). Testing the widest version means every flagged hour falls outside all of them, so no state’s count is overstated by this choice; a state’s own narrower window could only make its count higher.

§ 6WHAT WOULD CHANGE

Advisory hours that move with the forecast.

The forecasts are public, the WBGT formula is a 2008 open standard, and the occupational limits have been in NIOSH guidance since 1986. A city’s heat advisory could name hours that move with the forecast instead of a fixed afternoon.

Read this before citing a number

  • These are forecasts. Every “today” figure is a weather-model forecast (Open-Meteo, ~9–13 km grid over India), and WBGT is estimated from it with the Liljegren 2008 method. No instrument is read. Street-level conditions can differ; dense built-up areas typically run hotter than their grid cell, which makes these counts conservative there.
  • Lower bound by construction. The 11am–5pm window is the union of the audited state windows. Any real state window is narrower, so its own overlooked count could only be higher.
  • The limit. NIOSH’s recommended exposure limit (REL) for acclimatized workers, the right line for India’s chronically heat-exposed outdoor workforce. It is a per-hour working limit; this page invents no hours-per-day threshold.
  • The sample. 50 large cities, chosen for population coverage. No figure here is a national estimate. District worker counts are Census 2011; e-Shram registrations are 2021–present.
  • No “safe hours.” Hours below the limit are “lower-risk under estimated-WBGT assumptions.”
