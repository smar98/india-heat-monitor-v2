THE OVERLOOKED HOURS
Live · updated … Methods & sources Source code

A live evidence brief · 50 Indian cities · forecast, refreshed every 3 hours

The advice says work the morning and evening. The heat doesn't agree.

India's heat advisories tell outdoor workers to avoid the afternoon and move work to the cooler ends of the day. This page checks that advice against the forecast, hour by hour — and counts the recommended morning and evening hours that are themselves over the occupational heat-stress limit. Those are the overlooked hours.

§ 1THE RULE

The advice being tested here is official, written policy.

State Heat Action Plans widen that window — Odisha’s starts at 11am, Gujarat’s runs to 5pm. This page tests the widest audited version, 11am–5pm: every hour it flags falls outside even the most generous advisory on paper, so the counts below are a conservative lower bound. The implied promise of the advice is that the hours outside that window are workable. The rest of this page checks that promise.

§ 2THE FINDING · FORECAST TODAY

What counts as an overlooked hour — all three at once:
Outside 11am–5pm — an hour the advice points workers to Sun up — daylight, real solar load Over the limit — forecast WBGT at or above the NIOSH work limit for the selected workload

WBGT (wet-bulb globe temperature) is the standard occupational heat-stress measure — one number folding together heat, humidity, sun and wind, estimated here from public forecast data. The NIOSH limit slides with how hard the work is: heavier work makes more body heat, so its limit is lower. Hot nights over the limit are counted separately, never in the headline.

§ 7WHAT WOULD CHANGE

A fixed clock window can’t follow the heat. A forecast can.

None of the inputs here are exotic: the forecasts are public, the WBGT formula is a 2008 open standard, and the occupational limits have been in NIOSH guidance since 1986. A city’s heat advisory could name hours that move with the forecast rather than a fixed afternoon — this page is a working demonstration that the arithmetic is already possible with public data.

Read this before citing a number

  • Forecast, not observation. Every “today” figure is a weather-model forecast (Open-Meteo, ~9–13 km grid over India); WBGT is estimated from it (Liljegren 2008 method), not read off an instrument. A street-level reading can differ; dense built-up areas are typically hotter than their grid cell, which makes these counts conservative there.
  • Lower bound by construction. The 11am–5pm window is the union of the audited state windows; any real state window is narrower, so its overlooked count could only be higher.
  • The limit. NIOSH’s recommended exposure limit (REL) for acclimatized workers — the right line for India’s chronically heat-exposed outdoor workforce. It is a per-hour working limit; no hours-per-day threshold is invented here.
  • The sample. 50 large cities, chosen for population coverage — not a national estimate. District worker figures are Census 2011.
  • No “safe hours.” Hours below the limit are “lower-risk under estimated-WBGT assumptions,” never “safe.”
