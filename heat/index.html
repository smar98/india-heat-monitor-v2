Heat guidance shifts work into hours that cross the heat-stress limit in … cities today

India's heat guidance tells outdoor workers to shift into the morning and evening. On hot days, those hours can cross the heat-stress limit just as the afternoon does — the guidance shifts workers out of one risky window and into another.

Why this matters — and how to read this dashboard

The problem. India's official heat-wave alerts are declared when maximum air temperature crosses set thresholds (40°C in the plains, and so on), and state Heat Action Plans tell outdoor workers to avoid a fixed afternoon window. Both are anchored to temperature and the clock. But the body doesn't overheat by those alone: it cools itself by evaporating sweat, and humidity throttles that; sun and wind shift the load further. Two places at the same temperature — or the same hour of day — can carry very different physiological risk.

What this dashboard does. For ~50 large Indian cities it estimates WBGT — an occupational heat-stress metric that folds humidity, sun, and wind into one number — hour by hour, and counts the hours that exceed the NIOSH heat-stress reference limit outside the afternoon window guidance says to avoid. Those overlooked morning and evening hours are the finding: the "shift your work to the shoulders" advice can send workers into hours that carry higher heat-stress risk than the limit allows.

Why WBGT, if India doesn't use it officially? WBGT is the internationally standard occupational heat-stress metric (ISO 7243), used by militaries, sports bodies, and workplace-safety regulators worldwide, and Indian researchers have measured real heat strain in Indian workers around these limits. It isn't part of India's official heat-warning vocabulary yet — which is part of the point: this estimates it from public weather data to show what that vocabulary currently leaves out.

What to look for. Pick a workload — heavier work has a lower heat-stress limit — and read the count of cities with overlooked hours today. The map sizes each city by how many, and the workday clock shows one city hour by hour, with the afternoon window shaded and the overlooked shoulder hours in bright orange. Which cities top the list shifts day to day with the weather; that instability is why this runs live rather than as a one-off chart.

What this does not claim. No "safe hours," no street-level precision (the underlying data is a ~25km grid), and no measured WBGT — it's estimated from weather variables. Full formulas, sources, and caveats on the methods page.

Glossary — the terms this page uses
Maximum temperature (the IMD heat-wave trigger)
The daily peak of ordinary air temperature. India's official heat-wave alerts are declared when it crosses set thresholds (40°C in the plains, 37°C on the coast, and so on) — the main trigger of the official system.
Wet-bulb temperature
How hot it is once humidity is counted — a measure of how well sweating can still cool you. Always at or below the ordinary air temperature; the closer the two, the harder your body has to work.
Estimated WBGT
Wet Bulb Globe Temperature: the standard measure of heat stress for people working outdoors, combining heat, humidity, direct sun, and wind. "Estimated" because it's computed from weather data here, not read off a physical instrument.
Heat-stress limit (REL)
The WBGT above which continuous outdoor work gets risky, from NIOSH. It slides with how hard the work is — heavier work, lower limit — which is why there's a workload selector. Shown for acclimatized workers, the right setting for India's outdoor laborers.
Avoidance window & shoulder hours
Heat guidance says avoid the afternoon (we use the widest state version, 11am–5pm) and work the morning/evening "shoulders" instead. This dashboard flags shoulder hours that cross the heat-stress limit anyway — the hours the advice overlooks.
1991–2020 normal
What's typical for this exact calendar date in that city, averaged over 30 years — so "hot for July in Kolkata" means hotter than Kolkata's own usual July, not hotter than some all-India average.

Historical reanalysis and the live forecast are different data products — never read this chart against today's numbers. Model estimates, not observations. Same city and workload as selected above.

“Outside the window” uses 11am–5pm — the widest afternoon-avoidance window any audited state Heat Action Plan uses (Odisha starts 11:00, Gujarat ends 17:00; IMD's national advice is 12–3). So these hours fall outside even the most generous version of the guidance — the count is a conservative lower bound. The limit is NIOSH's REL for acclimatized workers at the selected workload; after-dark exceedances are humidity-driven and reported separately, never headlined. Counts are forecast for this 50-city sample, not a national estimate. Sources, formulas, and error bounds: methods page.

Estimated WBGT and wet-bulb temperature are model outputs computed from standard meteorological variables in gridded forecast/reanalysis data (Open-Meteo) — not station readings, and not physical measurements. Language on this site describes "lower-risk outdoor work windows under estimated WBGT assumptions" — never "safe hours."
