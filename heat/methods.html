The central question

India's Heat Action Plans tell outdoor workers to avoid the afternoon (roughly 11am–5pm across states) and shift work to the morning and evening. This dashboard asks: how often do those shifted-to shoulder hours themselves cross the occupational heat-stress limit, once humidity, sun, and wind are counted? Serious tools exist for occupational heat stress, thermal comfort, and live weather — but there is no public, India-specific, hourly view of outdoor-work heat stress set against the fixed work-hour guidance. This dashboard exists to fill that specific gap — not to claim no one tracks heat risk in India, which official IMD/NDMA heat-wave communication clearly does.

Three metrics, kept strictly separate

These are different physical quantities with different risk meanings. This dashboard never compares wet-bulb values against WBGT-style thresholds, or vice versa.

1. Dry-bulb (air) temperature

The ordinary air temperature a thermometer reads, taken as-is from Open-Meteo. Its daily maximum is the physical quantity IMD's heat-wave declarations threshold on (IMD's own term is "maximum temperature," not "dry-bulb"). Here it is used only as the T input to the wet-bulb formula below and the WBGT model — not as a ranking the dashboard sets itself against.

2. Wet-bulb temperature — Stull (2011) approximation

Wet-bulb temperature reflects how effectively the body can cool itself by sweat evaporation; unlike dry-bulb temperature, it accounts for humidity. Computed here via the empirical approximation published in:

Stull, R., 2011: "Wet-Bulb Temperature from Relative Humidity and Air Temperature." Journal of Applied Meteorology and Climatology, 50(11), 2267–2269. doi:10.1175/JAMC-D-11-0143.1

Formula (T = dry-bulb temp in °C, RH = relative humidity in %): 

Tw = T·atan[0.151977·(RH + 8.313659)^0.5]
     + atan(T + RH) − atan(RH − 1.676331)
     + 0.00391838·RH^1.5·atan(0.023101·RH)
     − 4.686035

Validity range: RH 5–99%, T −20°C to 50°C (accuracy degrades in the combined very-low-humidity / very-cold corner of that range, which this dashboard's cities never approach). Error bounds: −1°C to +0.65°C, mean absolute error under 0.3°C. The approximation assumes standard sea-level (101.325 kPa) pressure. Most of the ~50 cities here are low-elevation, but not all: for elevated cities — Srinagar (~1,600m), Bengaluru (~900m), and a few others in the 400–700m band — the wet-bulb values shown are slightly less exact (the formula modestly overestimates wet-bulb as pressure falls below sea level). The estimated WBGT is unaffected by this particular caveat: the Liljegren model takes each city's actual surface pressure as an input.

3. Estimated WBGT — Liljegren et al. (2008) method

Wet Bulb Globe Temperature (WBGT) is the internationally standard metric for outdoor occupational heat-stress risk (ISO 7243), used by militaries, sports governing bodies, and workplace-safety regulators worldwide, and applied by Indian researchers in urban and occupational heat-stress studies. It is not part of India's official heat-warning vocabulary — which is precisely the gap this dashboard examines: estimating WBGT from public weather data shows, hour by hour, what maximum-temperature heat-wave criteria and fixed afternoon work-hour windows leave out. Unlike wet-bulb temperature alone, WBGT also incorporates radiant heat (solar radiation) and wind. True WBGT requires a physical instrument (a black globe thermometer) that this dashboard does not have access to. Instead, this is an estimate derived purely from standard meteorological variables (temperature, humidity, solar radiation, wind) — supplied here by gridded forecast/reanalysis data, not physical station instruments — using the method from:

Liljegren, J. C., R. A. Carhart, P. Lawday, S. Tschopp, and R. Sharp, 2008: "Modeling the Wet Bulb Globe Temperature Using Standard Meteorological Measurements." The Journal of Occupational and Environmental Hygiene, 5(10), 645–655. doi:10.1080/15459620802310770

The implementation here is a Python port of James Liljegren's original 1.1 C code (originally published by UChicago Argonne, LLC under contract with the U.S. Department of Energy), obtained via the MIT-licensed mdljts/wbgt R package wrapper. The final combining formula, taken directly from that source code: 

WBGT = 0.1·T_air + 0.2·T_globe + 0.7·T_natural_wet_bulb

Verification: rather than trust the port by inspection, the original C code (with its own demo driver) was compiled directly with gcc and run on two test cases (a hot/humid daytime scenario and a nighttime scenario). The Python port's output matched the compiled C binary's actual output to within about 0.02–0.1°C — consistent with ordinary floating-point precision differences, not a translation error. Both test cases are kept as permanent automated regression tests in the public repository.

Caveat: this is an estimate from meteorological inputs, not a physical measurement. Liljegren et al. (2008) report agreement with measured WBGT to within about 1°C at the U.S. sites they validated against; no equivalent validation against measured WBGT exists for this dashboard's Indian grid data, which is why every count here carries the ±1°C sensitivity band.

Outdoor-work risk reference — NIOSH RAL/REL

This dashboard does not use a flat "safe/moderate/ danger" WBGT scale. The commonly cited ACGIH and ISO 7243 threshold tables vary continuously by workload and work/rest duty cycle, and their exact values are not freely published (OSHA's Technical Manual notes the ACGIH heat-stress tables are copyrighted and not publicly available). So the limits here come from NIOSH's own public formula:

NIOSH, 2016: "Criteria for a Recommended Standard: Occupational Exposure to Heat and Hot Environments." DHHS (NIOSH) Publication 2016-106, Chapter 8.1 (equations, p.93–94) and Table 5-1 (cross-standard comparison, p.101). cdc.gov/niosh/docs/2016-106 

RAL [°C-WBGT] = 59.9 − 14.1·log10(M)   (unacclimatized workers)
REL [°C-WBGT] = 56.7 − 11.5·log10(M)   (acclimatized workers)

where M is the 1-hour time-weighted-average metabolic rate, in watts, for a "standard" 70kg/1.8m² worker doing continuous (60 min/h) work. The dashboard's workload selector uses four levels on NIOSH's own kcal/h basis (converted at 1 kcal/h = 1.163 W), and reports the REL (acclimatized) line by default. The same four values are used everywhere on the site:

WorkloadMetabolic rateREL (°C WBGT)Illustrative task
Light180 kcal/h ≈ 209 W30.0standing supervision, light assembly
Moderate300 kcal/h ≈ 349 W27.5brisk walking with a load, street vending
Heavy (default)400 kcal/h ≈ 465 W26.0digging, brick-carrying, most farm labour
Very heavy500 kcal/h ≈ 581 W24.9sustained shovelling, peak harvest/construction

The moderate row (349 W → REL 27.5°C) is the exact value NIOSH's own worked example uses. Occupation labels are illustrative task intensities, not fixed per-job values — the same job spans a wide range depending on the specific task. Why REL, not RAL: India's outdoor laborers are among the most chronically heat-acclimatized workers anywhere, and Indian occupational-health field studies measured real heat strain (core-temperature rise, dehydration markers) in such workers around these WBGT limits — so the acclimatized line is the defensible default, and RAL would understate their tolerance. See Venugopal et al., "Occupational Heat Stress Profiles in Selected Workplaces in India," Int. J. Environ. Res. Public Health, 2016 (PMC4730480), and related work on heat strain in Tamil Nadu salt-pan and agricultural workers (PMC10334398).

These are NOT flat "safe/danger" cutoffs. They vary continuously with workload, and depend on acclimatization, clothing, and work/rest scheduling — none of which this dashboard measures directly. Language on this site always says "lower-risk outdoor work windows under estimated WBGT assumptions," never "safe hours," and every threshold shown is labeled with the specific NIOSH reference it corresponds to.

Data sources

Live forecast

Open-Meteo Forecast API: hourly temperature, relative humidity, shortwave solar radiation, wind speed, and surface pressure for all ~50 cities, refreshed automatically every 3 hours.

City list

50 large Indian cities: the top 50 by population, with exactly three substitutions, named so the choice can be audited — Kota, Bareilly, and Solapur (each climatically redundant with cities already on the list) were swapped for Bhubaneswar, Kochi, and Puducherry, so humid coastal and eastern India are represented rather than a strict population cutoff drying out the sample. The swaps were made when the list was built, before any results existed, and at most those three cities of any day's count can be substitutes — they cannot drive a headline that moves by tens of cities. India state/UT boundaries from the DataMeet India community's "maps" repository (MIT licensed), simplified for faster loading with all boundary shapes otherwise unmodified (license/attribution).

Official heat alerts (logged, not yet published)

Since July 2026, each data refresh also records which official heat alerts are active for each monitored city, from two public sources: the India Meteorological Department's Common Alerting Protocol (CAP) feed (IMD-signed alerts carrying the actual warning areas) and NDMA's SACHET national alert portal (which also carries state-SDMA alerts). The record is kept so a future update can compare official warnings against these estimates using real alerts rather than a reconstruction of declaration criteria. No such comparison is published yet.

What "overlooked hours" means

For each city, every hour of today is classified against the NIOSH REL heat-stress limit for the selected workload (acclimatized workers). An hour is an overlooked hour when it crosses that limit, falls outside the 11am–5pm afternoon-avoidance window, and has the sun up (shortwave radiation above ~50 W/m²). Those are the morning and evening “shoulder” hours that guidance tells workers to shift into — yet on hot days they can carry real, solar-driven heat stress. Hours that cross the limit after dark are counted and reported separately, because after sunset WBGT's globe/solar term is near zero, so a high night value is essentially humid heat (wet-bulb) — which IMD's “warm night” category already speaks to.

The city count's bar is one hour. A city enters the headline count with a single overlooked hour, and the KPI label says so, alongside how many cities have three or more. That three-hour figure is descriptive, not a risk threshold: NIOSH's REL is a ceiling for each hour of continuous work (a 1-hour time-weighted average) — the criteria document prescribes work/rest cycling and controls whenever an hour exceeds it, and defines no hours-per-day dose above which risk steps up. Inventing such a cutoff here would be unsourced, so depth of exposure is reported as plain counts (total city-hours, the per-city clock) instead.

The 11am–5pm window is the union of the documented outdoor-work windows in the table below: the earliest start any of them uses (Odisha, 11:00) to the latest end (Delhi and Ahmedabad, 17:00). Using that union makes the overlooked-hours count a conservative lower bound: an hour flagged as overlooked falls outside every one of these documented windows, so under any single state's (narrower) window the count could only be higher. The claim is scoped to the windows audited here, not to every HAP in India. The per-city policy card shows each audited state's window as written, but computing the count against each city's own (narrower) window is deferred to a later version — the union is the conservative choice.

Windows are stated as clock hours; the dashboard's hourly cells are labelled by the hour they begin in IST, and Open-Meteo's solar input for an hour is the mean over the preceding hour — so a cell at, say, 5 pm reflects roughly 4–5 pm conditions. Boundary hours can therefore shift a count by an hour either way; the headline figure should be read as approximate, not to-the-hour exact.

Which cities have the most overlooked hours changes day to day with the weather — because estimated WBGT weighs sun and wind, some days it is inland northern cities, other days humid coastal ones. This dashboard does not claim any fixed set of cities is always underrated; the claim is that fixed afternoon-only work-hour guidance systematically misses solar-driven stress in the shoulder hours, and a live view is the only way to show where and when.

Do the plans have teeth? The CPR 2023 assessment

The dashboard's per-city policy card and map popups cite the Centre for Policy Research's March 2023 review, How Is India Adapting to Heatwaves?, which analysed 37 heat action plans (9 city, 13 district, 15 state, published 2016–2022). Two of its findings are quoted on the dashboard, both from the executive summary (p. 3):

  • “HAPs are underfunded. Only 11 of 37 HAPs discuss funding sources. Of these, eight asked implementing departments to self-allocate resources, indicating a serious funding constraint.”
  • “HAPs have weak legal foundations. None of the HAPs indicate the legal sources of their authority.”

CPR reports these findings in aggregate and does not publish a per-plan scorecard, so the dashboard shows them as context about India's heat plans overall — never as a grade of any specific plan quoted above. The policy card is descriptive and display-only: the computed avoidance window everywhere on this site remains the 11am–5pm union described earlier, regardless of what any single plan says.

The long view: overlooked hours per year, 1980–2024

The trend panel applies exactly the same definition and code path as today's count — Stull wet-bulb, the Liljegren WBGT model, the NIOSH REL for the selected workload, the 11am–5pm window, the sun-up rule — to 45 years of hourly history, and counts the overlooked hours in each calendar year (IST).

Data. Hourly ERA5 reanalysis (temperature, relative humidity, shortwave radiation, wind, surface pressure) from the Open-Meteo Historical Weather API. Computed once and published as a static dataset; the script that builds it is in the public repository.

Why 1980, not 1940. ERA5 extends back to 1940, but its pre-satellite-era quality is weakest in exactly the variables WBGT leans on most — solar radiation and wind. Starting at 1980 keeps the whole series inside the satellite era; 45 years is ample for the trend question.

Why 2025 is excluded. It is incomplete: a half year would render as a collapse in an annual-count chart. The series ends with the last complete year, 2024.

The window anachronism, stated plainly. The 11am–5pm avoidance window is today's audited HAP union applied to every year since 1980 — the chart answers “how many hours would today's guidance overlook,” not “what did guidance overlook at the time” (most of the period predates any HAP; the first Indian HAP is Ahmedabad's, 2013).

Two data products, never mixed. The live dashboard runs on forecast data; the trend runs on reanalysis. They differ in kind (a reanalysis assimilates observations after the fact) and the page never reads one against the other — the trend panel carries its own caveat saying so.

Computation notes: the sensitivity band shown is the same ±1°C WBGT-error convention as the headline. Hours with missing variables are dropped, not filled; hours provably too cool to approach any limit are counted as below it (the derivation of that bound is documented in the build script); solver non-convergence yields no estimate, not a zero. All counts are model estimates on a ~25 km grid, not observations.

The district layer: workers at risk

The "Workers at risk, by district" map layer answers a different question than the city dots: not just where is it hot, but how many outdoor workers does the advice overlook, and where. Each of the 640 districts (2011 Census frame) is shaded by worker-hours at risk: its outdoor workforce multiplied by today's forecast overlooked hours at the selected workload.

Workforce data. Census of India 2011, table B-04 (main workers by industrial category), per district. "Outdoor workers" counts main workers in the predominantly-outdoor categories: NIC A (cultivators, agricultural labourers, and plantation/livestock/forestry/fishing), NIC B (mining), and NIC F (construction) — about 212 million people nationally. This deliberately under-counts outdoor exposure (street vendors sit in retail, brick-kiln workers partly in manufacturing); erring low is the honest direction. The per-state totals were verified to equal the sum of their districts exactly, and every mapped district joins a workforce record. (State-level registration counts under the BOCW Act, published on data.gov.in as of March 2024, offer a more recent — though registration-based and state-level — view of the construction workforce.)

Heat data. One forecast point per district (a representative interior point), refreshed once daily in the early morning IST, scored with the same estimated-WBGT-vs-REL definition as everything else on this page. The layer is labeled with its data date if a refresh is missed.

Boundaries. 2011 Census district boundaries from the DataMeet India community (simplified for the browser; shapes otherwise unmodified — license/attribution). The 2011 frame is used deliberately so the Census join is exact; districts created after 2011 appear within their parent boundaries.

Read the shading as scale of exposure, not precision: the workforce shares are from 2011 (India's last completed census — occupational structure moves slowly, but it is 15 years old); the heat value is one model grid point (~25 km) per district, and large districts span more microclimates than one point can carry; and the hours are forecast, not observed. Worker-hours multiply those uncertainties together — the layer ranks districts against each other; it does not measure any district's true exposure.

The e-Shram registry cross-check

Because the census workforce shares are 15 years old, the map offers a second, recent view: the “Outdoor workforce — e-Shram registry” layer shades each district by its registrations on e-Shram, the Ministry of Labour and Employment's national registry of unorganised workers, in the two predominantly-outdoor occupation sectors it records (agriculture and construction). Counts come from the ministry's district-wise dataset on data.gov.in, refreshed daily by the ministry; each district's figure is an exact filtered count of individual registration records, queried — not estimated — from the full registry.

e-Shram reports today's ~780 districts; the map uses the 640 districts of the 2011 census. We fold every post-2011 district back into its 2011 parent using a documented rename-and-split table (205 entries in the public build script) — things like Prayagraj→Allahabad, Gurugram→Gurgaon, and Karnataka's 2014 renames (Bengaluru←Bangalore, Mysuru←Mysore, and others). All 640 census districts join: 100% coverage, 0% of registrations unmatched.

Telangana is a special case. Created in 2014, it does not exist in the 2011 census frame: its registrations are counted under the undivided Andhra Pradesh districts they were carved from, so the polygon labelled “Warangal, Andhra Pradesh” on this layer includes today's Warangal Urban, Warangal Rural, Mahabubabad, Jayashankar Bhupalpally, Jangaon, and Mulugu — the map popup says so for every affected district. Separately, 22 newer districts nationwide were carved from more than one 2011 parent; each is assigned wholly to its majority parent, a modeling choice that totals about 1% of national registrations, too small to move the map.

The point of the layer is the cross-check itself: district-for-district, the current registration geography agrees with the Census-2011 outdoor-workforce map at a rank correlation of about 0.81 (recomputed at every build and shown on the layer) — broad agreement, not identity, and evidence that the worker-hours risk layer is not leaning on a stale picture of where outdoor workers are. One caution in reading that number: both series are absolute counts, so part of the agreement is district size — big districts have more of everything. The check is still the right one for this layer, which ranks districts by absolute worker-hours; the correlation says that ranking would look broadly the same against the recent registry.

Registrations are not a workforce count. e-Shram covers unorganised workers aged 16–59 who have registered since 2021 — enrollment is incentive-driven and its completeness varies by state — and it excludes the organised/formal workforce (which is why mining, largely formal, is not part of this layer's definition). That is also why the layer is read by rank correlation, not by level: the risk layer's weights remain the Census 2011 main-worker counts; the registry layer is a corroborating view, never an input to any computed number.

AI-transparency note

This dashboard's data pipeline, scientific-formula implementations, and frontend code were built with AI assistance (Claude, Anthropic), working from a project brief and editorial decisions set by the site's author. Where that matters for trusting the numbers on this page:

  • Every formula was checked against a primary source (the original paper or standard, or a citation of it that could itself be verified) rather than accepted from a first-pass summary. Where a primary source could not be reached (the ACGIH/ISO 7243 exact tables, which are genuinely paywalled), that gap is stated above rather than papered over with an invented number presented as authoritative.
  • The Liljegren WBGT port was verified by compiling the original author's C code directly and comparing outputs numerically, not just by reading the ported code and judging it plausible.
  • All reproducible pipeline scripts (data fetching, the wet-bulb and WBGT calculations, the historical builders) are in the public repository, along with an automated test suite.
